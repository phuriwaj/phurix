---
title: "Progressive Sharpening"
date: 2024-04-18
topics: ["design", "tools"]
lede: "Features start optional, become ergonomic for the curious, then essential to the power user — the progression runs uphill, never the reverse."
---

Mature software has a shape: new users see a small surface, intermediates see a few keyboard shortcuts, and power users see a command bar. The progression runs uphill, never downhill. A feature that's essential on day one forces every new user to climb a wall they didn't know existed.

This pattern is the antidote. The shape of the surface should sharpen with the user, not against them.

## When to use it

- Tools with a stable core audience that grows in fluency.
- Anywhere a "simple mode" toggle has been proposed.

## When to avoid it

- Consumer products where convenience *now* is the only metric that matters.

## What breaks when you get it wrong

The most common failure mode is the well-meaning onboarding tour. "Here are the seven things you can do with this product." The new user, presented with seven doors, picks none of them and leaves. A power user, told about seven things they already know, resents the patronising email. Progressive sharpening means: at any given moment, show the user the doors that match their fluency. Don't show them the ones they haven't earned yet — they'll see those doors when they're ready to walk through them.
